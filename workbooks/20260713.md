## テーマ: Sustainable Urbanism: The Rise of 15-Minute Cities

### 英文パッセージ

The concept of the "15-minute city," originally proposed by Professor Carlos Moreno, has rapidly shifted from a niche urban planning theory to a cornerstone of modern sustainable urbanism. At its core, the model envisions a decentralized city structure where all essential human needs—work, housing, food, healthcare, education, and recreation—can be accessed within a 15-minute walk or bicycle ride from one's residence. By prioritizing proximity and accessibility, this framework aims to drastically reduce individual carbon footprints by curbing reliance on private automobiles, which remain a primary contributor to urban air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions.

However, the implementation of such a model is not without its complexities. Critics often point to the potential for "green gentrification," where the enhancement of local amenities drives up property values, inadvertently displacing the very low-income residents the model seeks to support. Furthermore, transitioning existing, sprawling metropolises designed around highways into compact, pedestrian-friendly hubs requires significant infrastructural overhaul and political will. There is also the challenge of socio-economic polarization; if not managed equitably, 15-minute neighborhoods could become isolated bubbles of privilege rather than integrated parts of a cohesive city.

Proponents argue that the benefits—ranging from improved mental health due to reduced commute times to increased local economic resilience—far outweigh these hurdles. As global cities like Paris and Melbourne experiment with these policies, the 15-minute city represents a fundamental reimagining of the urban experience, seeking to reconcile the demands of modern efficiency with the imperative of environmental stewardship.
