## テーマ: The Concept of "Degrowth" as a Challenge to Traditional Economic Paradigms

### 英文パッセージ

In the face of an accelerating climate crisis and widening socio-economic disparities, the traditional paradigm of perpetual economic growth is being increasingly scrutinized. While mainstream economic discourse has long equated a rising Gross Domestic Product (GDP) with societal progress, proponents of the "degrowth" movement argue that this model is fundamentally incompatible with the ecological limits of a finite planet. Degrowth is not to be confused with a mere recession; rather, it is a planned, democratic reduction of production and consumption in affluent nations, aimed at achieving social justice and ecological sustainability.

At its core, degrowth challenges the hegemony of "productivism"—the entrenched belief that more production is inherently better. Critics of the current system argue that our economic engine relies on the continuous extraction of resources and the generation of waste, a process that has already overshot several "planetary boundaries." By decoupling human well-being from economic expansion, degrowth advocates for a transition toward a "steady-state economy." This involves prioritizing "use-value" over "exchange-value," fostering communal sharing, and shortening the working week to redistribute labor and leisure more equitably.

Opponents, however, contend that degrowth is a utopian fantasy that could lead to mass unemployment and technological stagnation. They advocate instead for "green growth," suggesting that innovation and renewable energy can decouple growth from environmental degradation. Yet, the empirical evidence for such absolute decoupling remains contentious. As the debate intensifies, the central question remains: can humanity redefine prosperity before the structural imperatives of growth lead to systemic collapse?
