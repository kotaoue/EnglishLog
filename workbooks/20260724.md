## テーマ: The Concept of "Transgenerational Justice" in Climate Ethics

### 英文パッセージ

The concept of "transgenerational justice" has emerged as a pivotal framework in ethical discourse, particularly concerning climate change and resource depletion. At its core, it posits that the present generation holds a fiduciary responsibility to act as stewards of the Earth, ensuring that future inhabitants inherit a planet capable of sustaining human flourishing. This paradigm shifts the focus from immediate socio-economic utility to a long-term temporal horizon, challenging the traditional "discount rates" often employed in economic modeling, which tend to undervalue the well-being of those yet to be born.

The realization of transgenerational justice faces significant hurdles, primarily rooted in systemic inertia and the "tyranny of the present." Political systems, often tethered to short-term electoral cycles, prioritize immediate gratification and economic growth over the mitigation of slow-onset environmental catastrophes. Furthermore, the legal standing of future generations remains a subject of intense debate. While some jurisdictions have begun to recognize the "rights of nature" or the constitutional rights of descendants to a stable climate, the enforcement of such principles requires a radical restructuring of global governance.

Ultimately, embracing transgenerational justice necessitates a recalibration of our moral compass. It requires us to acknowledge that our current lifestyle, fueled by finite resources and carbon-intensive processes, imposes an "ecological debt" on future generations. Addressing this inequity is not merely an act of altruism but a fundamental requirement for the continuity of civilization. By integrating long-term foresight into policy-making, we can begin to bridge the gap between contemporary demands and the survival needs of our successors.
