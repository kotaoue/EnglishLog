## テーマ: Biomimicry: Nature as a Blueprint for Sustainable Innovation

### 英文パッセージ

Biomimicry, a term derived from the Greek words *bios* (life) and *mimesis* (imitation), is an approach to innovation that seeks sustainable solutions to human challenges by emulating nature's time-tested patterns and strategies. Instead of merely exploiting natural resources, biomimicry views nature as a mentor and a measure. For billions of years, life on Earth has evolved through a process of trial and error, resulting in highly efficient systems and structures that operate seamlessly within planetary boundaries.

One of the most celebrated examples of biomimicry is the redesign of Japan's Shinkansen bullet train. The train’s original design caused a loud "tunnel boom" due to atmospheric pressure changes when entering tunnels. By modeling the front of the train after the beak of a kingfisher—a bird that can dive into water with minimal splash—engineers reduced noise, improved aerodynamics, and increased energy efficiency by 15%. Similarly, the serrated edges on wind turbine blades were inspired by the tubercles on humpback whale fins, which allow these massive creatures to move with surprising agility by reducing drag.

This shift in perspective represents a fundamental change in the human-nature relationship. Modern industrial processes have long been characterized by a "heat, beat, and treat" approach, utilizing extreme temperatures and toxic chemicals to manufacture goods. In contrast, nature manufactures at ambient temperatures, uses sunlight for energy, and views "waste" as a resource for other organisms. By integrating biological principles into engineering and urban design, biomimicry offers a pathway toward a regenerative economy—one that does not merely minimize harm but actively contributes to the health of the biosphere.
