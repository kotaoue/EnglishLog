## テーマ: The Concept of "Epistemic Humility" in the Age of Information Abundance

### 英文パッセージ

In an era characterized by the democratization of information, the concept of "epistemic humility" has emerged as a vital intellectual virtue. It refers to the recognition of the inherent limitations of one’s own knowledge and the potential for error in one’s beliefs. While the internet provides instantaneous access to a myriad of data points, this accessibility often fosters an "illusion of explanatory depth," where individuals mistake familiarity with a subject for true expertise. 

This cognitive overconfidence is exacerbated by algorithmic echo chambers that reinforce existing biases, leading to increased social polarization. Epistemic humility, however, does not imply a lack of conviction or a descent into radical skepticism. Rather, it involves a proactive openness to new evidence and an acknowledgment of the complexity of truth. It requires what philosophers call "intellectual fallibility"—the willingness to admit that one might be wrong even when one feels most certain.

Cultivating this mindset is essential for both scientific inquiry and healthy democratic discourse. By embracing the boundaries of our understanding, we become more adept at navigating the nuances of modern crises, from climate change to artificial intelligence ethics. Ultimately, the practice of epistemic humility serves as a safeguard against dogmatism, encouraging a more collaborative and rigorous pursuit of knowledge in an increasingly fragmented world.
