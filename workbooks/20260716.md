## テーマ: Anthropocentrism and the Shift Towards Biocentric Conservation

### 英文パッセージ

Historically, the discourse surrounding environmental conservation has been predominantly anchored in anthropocentrism—a philosophical viewpoint that interprets the world through the lens of human values and experiences. From this perspective, nature is often appraised based on its "utilitarian value," meaning its worth is derived solely from the benefits it provides to humanity, such as ecosystem services, carbon sequestration, or medicinal resources. While this pragmatic approach has successfully mobilized economic incentives for preservation, critics argue that it reinforces a hierarchical relationship where non-human entities are relegated to the status of mere instruments.

In recent years, however, there has been a significant paradigm shift toward "biocentrism," which posits that all living beings possess inherent worth, independent of their utility to humans. This perspective challenges the long-standing assumption that human interests should invariably take precedence in policy-making. For instance, the legal movement to grant "personhood" to rivers and forests reflects a radical departure from traditional property laws, suggesting that the environment is a subject with its own rights rather than an object to be managed.

The tension between these two ideologies lies at the heart of modern ecological debates. Proponents of a biocentric approach contend that as long as conservation is tied to human profit, it remains vulnerable to market fluctuations and technological shifts. Conversely, pragmatists argue that in a world driven by capital, assigning an abstract intrinsic value to nature may fail to provide the immediate political leverage necessary for urgent action. Navigating this dichotomy requires a nuanced synthesis—recognizing our profound interdependence with the biosphere while fostering an ethical stewardship that transcends narrow self-interest.
