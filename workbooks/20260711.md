## テーマ: The Impact of Cognitive Biases on Professional Decision-Making

### 英文パッセージ

The human brain is an extraordinary organ, yet it is far from a perfect processor of information. In the realm of behavioral economics and psychology, "cognitive biases" represent systematic patterns of deviation from norm or rationality in judgment. These mental shortcuts, often referred to as heuristics, allow individuals to make rapid decisions in complex environments. However, while they were evolutionarily advantageous for immediate survival, they frequently lead to perceptual distortion, inaccurate judgment, or illogical interpretation in the modern professional landscape.

One prevalent example is "confirmation bias," the tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information in a way that confirms one's preexisting beliefs. In a corporate setting, this can manifest as a leadership team disregarding data that contradicts their current strategy, potentially leading to catastrophic financial oversight. Another significant phenomenon is the "sunk cost fallacy," where individuals continue an endeavor as a result of previously invested resources, such as time or money, even when it is clear that the current costs outweigh any potential future benefits. This fallacy often tethers organizations to failing projects, preventing the reallocation of capital to more promising ventures.

To mitigate these inherent biases, organizations are increasingly adopting "debiasing" strategies. This involves fostering a culture of psychological safety where dissenting opinions are encouraged, and implementing structured decision-making frameworks that necessitate the consideration of alternative perspectives. By acknowledging our psychological fallibility, we can strive toward more objective and data-driven outcomes, ensuring that our intuitive leaps do not lead us off a metaphorical cliff.
