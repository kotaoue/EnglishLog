## テーマ: The Ethical Implications of De-extinction and Biodiversity

### 英文パッセージ

The prospect of "de-extinction"—the process of resurrecting extinct species through advanced genomic tools like CRISPR—has shifted from the realm of science fiction to a tangible, albeit controversial, scientific pursuit. Proponents argue that bringing back keystone species, such as the woolly mammoth or the passenger pigeon, could restore degraded ecosystems and enhance biodiversity. For instance, the "mammoth steppe" hypothesis suggests that reintroducing mammoth-like hybrids to the Arctic could help sequester carbon by maintaining the permafrost, thereby mitigating climate change.

However, the ethical and ecological ramifications remain profoundly divisive. Critics contend that de-extinction is a hubristic endeavor that distracts from the urgent necessity of preserving currently endangered species. There is a concern known as "moral hazard": if the public perceives extinction as reversible, the impetus to protect existing habitats might diminish. Furthermore, the ecological niche once occupied by these species has often irrevocably changed. Reintroducing a long-lost organism into a modern ecosystem could result in unforeseen consequences, potentially turning a resurrected species into an invasive one that destabilizes existing biotic communities.

Ultimately, de-extinction compels us to interrogate our relationship with nature. Is our role to be masters of the biological clock, or humble stewards of the extant world? While the technical feasibility continues to improve, the philosophical question remains: just because we *can* bring a species back, *should* we? The allocation of limited conservation resources remains a zero-sum game, and the allure of high-tech "charismatic megafauna" must be weighed against the pragmatic needs of today’s fragile ecosystems.
